Introduction

Currently, hundreds of thousands of people are waiting for an organ transplant., but unfortunately, the world does not have enough donors to accommodate all the patients. And this is where the bioprinting of human tissue comes in. 3D bioprinting is an emerging branch of regenerative medicine, it is similar to 3D printing. However, bioprinting uses bio-ink, a printable material that contains living cells. Materials or cells may be obtained from donors or, much better, from patients to lessen the chances of the body rejecting the printed tissue. Although printing thin tissues, like skin and meniscus, is the only successful project in the present, researchers are looking forward to the future of this technology.

Science Concepts Involved

Biology: Tissue printing is a 3D printing technology that involves creating three-dimensional structures of living cells and biomaterials to produce tissue-like constructs. The process usually involves depositing cells and supporting biomaterials layer by layer using specialized printers, and the cells are carefully placed in a specific pattern to create a desired structure. In order to actually print tissue, we need biomaterials. Biomaterials are materials that were designed to interact with different biological systems, such as cells, tissues, and organs, either in vivo (work performed in a living organism) or in vitro (work performed outside of the living organism). Biomaterials can either be synthetic or natural in origin, and are used in a variety of real-world applications in the fields of medicine and biotechnology. Theyâ€™re typically characterized by their ability to support cell adhesion and proliferation, and to facilitate tissue growth and regeneration. They may also be designed to mimic the structural and mechanical properties of natural tissues and organs, or to deliver therapeutic agents like drugs or growth factors. Some examples of common biomaterials include; Polymer Synthetic, which are commonly used as biomaterials die to their biocompatibility and tunable mechanical properties; Ceramics, which are used as bone graft substitutes due to their ability to promote bone growth and regeneration; Natural materials are another commonly used biomaterial due to their biocompatibility and ability to mimic the structural and mechanical properties of natural tissues.

Chemistry: The use of bio-ink, which is made up of either natural or synthetic biomaterials, or a combination of both, with the use of three main components; cells that are used to actually make up the tissue, hydrogels which are water-based materials that provides a supportive matrix for the cells in the bioinks, and growth factors, which are proteins that stimulate the growth and differentiation of cells. These materials are bound through crosslinkers, which are chemicals that help solidify the hydrogel matrix and stabilize the printed structure. In order to preserve the cells, they undergo cryopreservation, a process where the cells are frozen at really low temperatures (typically using liquid nitrogen) to preserve them for an extended period of time.

Physics: 3D bioprinting of tissues uses methods such as extrusion, which uses air pressure to print the bio-ink; laser printers use focused lasers to cut the bio-ink. The physics of biological structure formation, including but not limited to the mechanisms by which tissues and organs form and self-assemble in the developing embryo, provides reliable tools to design bioprinting experiments and to predict the outcome of printing (A Path for Clinical Translation of 3D-bioprinted Human Tissues, 2022).
