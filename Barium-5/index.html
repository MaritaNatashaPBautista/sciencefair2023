Taking inspiration from nature

Each organism present has unique ways of manufacturing color. This study focuses on how these structural colors are a product of gradual changes over millions of years, brought about by evolution. Structural coloring is used by organisms as a form of adaptation to overcome hurdles in survival. This concept is evident in peacock feathers, beetle shells, butterfly wings, and many others.

The chemistry of plasmonic paint

This new paint doesn't use pigments but instead arranges nanoparticles of colorless metals like aluminum and aluminum oxide to create structural color. The paint contains aluminum flakes with small clusters of aluminum atoms, which vibrate and resonate when exposed to white light, producing a single color that depends on the size of the nanoparticle. This happens because the free electrons in metallic materials cause the nanoparticles to oscillate, returning light as a single visible color.

How light travels in the paint

This project uses plasmonic nanoparticles that support LSPRs (localized surface plasmon resonances). These oscillations of electrons generate strong electric fields that can interact with molecules and other particles, improving light absorption, scattering, and emission. By implementing these particles on a surface, it creates a controlled interaction with light, which could lead to ultralight plasmonic paint that minimizes weight by altering how light interacts with the surface.

LIGHTENS AIRCRAFT LOAD

from 500 kg to 1.3 kg

Planes have normally has 600-1,200 lbs of paint applied to it. If structural paint is used instead, this amount could be decreased by approximately a hundred times, which could allow the aircraft to accommodate more passengers, baggage, and cargo. To give a perspective, a Boeing 747 needs 500 kilograms of paint while this experimental paint only needs a mere 1.3 kilograms (Whitney, 2023). Reducing an aircraft’s weight can also improve fuel efficiency and save millions of dollars for the airline.

A LONG LASTING SOLUTION

from 15 yrs to 50+ yrs

Traditional pigments used in paint can break down over time when exposed to sunlight, leading to fading or discoloration of the painted object. However, the type of paint being discussed here - which uses structural color produced by nanoparticles - is different. It is believed that this paint may last much longer than traditional pigment-based paint due to the stability of the nanoparticle arrangement. In fact, it is possible that objects painted with this type of paint could retain their color for decades without needing to be repainted.

REDUCES CITY HEAT

from 37°C to 21°C

Another exclusive property of this paint is its inability to absorb infrared radiation, unlike traditional paints which trap heat. This can lead to surfaces being 20 to 30 degrees Fahrenheit cooler than normal. Consequently, this keeps buildings cooler without the need for indoor cooling, saving considerable amounts of energy. A widespread use of this paint in cities could potentially lead to the local climate of the area decreasing in temperature, mitigating the “urban heat island” effect that plague many urban areas worldwide.
