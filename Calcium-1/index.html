Introduction

With the world constantly evolving, there is a constant need for new and better technologies. With these innovations, there also must be a constant supply of energy to power them. Fossil fuels are doing the job well, but these nonrenewable energy supplies will run out sooner or later.

Solar panels are a good renewable alternative as an energy source, as these are "enough to handle the entire world's energy consumption for a full year" (n.d.). Instead of relying on non-renewable resources to power inventions, these panels are already well-known and proven to be effective.

The design of The California Institute of Technology (CIT) and Karlsruhe Institute of Technology (KIT)'s solar panels are based on the pattern and design of the black rose butterfly or Pachliopta aristolochiae which utilizes a semiconductor material of thin film with sheets of hydrogenated amorphous silicon, topped with a layer of minuscule holes with varying sizes.

Yirka (2017) mentions that normal thin-film solar cells are known to be less expensive, lighter, and flexible, although they perform less efficiently than traditional crystal-based cells. In order to relieve this, the application of the butterfly-wing-inspired nanostructures aims to improve the thin-film cells' efficiency.
